1 Key Concepts & Definitions

A list of c&d you need to know before you start

Service

A means of enabling valjue co-creation by facilitating outcomes that customer want to achive, without the customer having to manage specific costs and risks.

Value & Co-creation

The perceived benefits, usefulness, and importance of something.

Value is co-created through an active collaboration between providers and consumers.

Costs & Risks

Cost: The amount of money spent on a specific activity or resource.

Risk: A possible event that could cause harm or loss. Services remove risks from consumers, but also impose new risks on them.

Service management

A set of specialized organizational capabilities for enebling value for the customer in the form of service.

Utility vs Warranty

Utility (Fit for purpose): The functionality offered by a product or service to meet a particular need. (What the service does).

Warranty (Fit for use): Assurance that a product or service will meet agreed requirements. (Availability, Capacity, Security, Continuity).

Product & Resource

Product: a configuragion of an organization's resources designed to offer value for a customer. Can be used by one or more service offering.

Resource: something you own of have access to. People, information, technology, value streams, processes, suppliers, partners ecc... Resources can be combined toghether to create product delivered as a service to create value for the customers.

Outputs vs Outcomes

Output: A tangible or intangible deliverable of an activity (e.g., a wedding ring).

Outcome: A result for a stakeholder enabled by one or more outputs (e.g., a happy marriage).

Service Offerings

A description of one or more services, designed to address the needs of a target consumer group. Includes:

  • Goods: something that is transferred from the supplier to the consumer, so the cojsumer takes ownership
  • Access to resources: something that is granted or licensed to the consumer under specific termis and conditions, but ownership remain with the provider
  • Service actions: actions performed by the provider to support the needs of the consumer

Service relationship

a cooperation between a service provider and service consumer. Service relationship include:

  • service provisioning: activities performed by an organization to provide service and/or goods.
  • service consumption: the activities performed by a service consumer to utilize services, manage necessary resources, and request actions, aimed at co-creating value. It involves using the provider's resources and, where necessary, acquiring goods to achieve desired outcomes.
  • service relationship management: joint activities performed by a service provider and a service consumer to ensure continual value co-creation based on agreed and available service offering.

Provider & consumer

Service provider: an organization that delivers service to consumer within its own organization or externarl to its own organization.

Service consumer: an organization that uses the service provided to them y any organization with their own organization or external to their organization.


Organization: a person or group of people that has its own functions with responsibilities, authorities and relationship to achive its objective. Organizations can be both provider or consumer.

Consumer Key Roles

  • Sponsor: Authorizes budget for service consumption.
  • Customer: Defines requirements for a service and takes responsibility for outcomes.
  • User: A person who uses services.

Stakeholders

a person or organization that has an interest or involvement in ah organization, product, service, practice or other entity.

Continual improvement

Show up multple time into ITIL:

PRACTICE: .

MODEL: a model providing a structured approach that can be adopted and adapted for implementing improvements.

ACTIVITY: activity that ensure a continual improvement of product, practice, resources, procedures and services across all value chain activities.

2 The 4 Dimensions of Service (VOIP)

To ensure a holistic approach, these 4 dimensions must be considered for every service.

Organizations & People

Formal organizational structures, culture, required staffing and competencies, roles, and responsibilities.

Information & Technology

The information and knowledge used to deliver services, and the technologies used to manage them (Workflow mgmt, KB, Cloud).

Partners & Suppliers

Relationships with other organizations that are involved in the design, development, deployment, delivery, and support of services.

Value Streams & Processes

How the various parts of the organization work in an integrated and coordinated way to enable value creation.

4 SERVICE VALUE SYSTEM

Service Value System (SVS)

Describes how all the components and activities of the organization work together as a system to enable value creation.

Input: Opportunity/Demand → Output: Value.

Guiding Principles Governance Service Value Chain Practices Continual Improvement

Service Value Chain (SVC)

The central element of the SVS. An operating model which outlines the key activities required to respond to demand and facilitate value realization. (Remember: PIEDOD)

Plan

Ensure a shared understanding of the vision, current status, and improvement direction for all four dimensions.

Improve

Ensure continual improvement of products, services, and practices across all value chain activities.

Engage

Provide a good understanding of stakeholder needs, transparency, and continual engagement (Interacting with users/customers).

Design & Transition

Ensure that products and services continually meet stakeholder expectations for quality, costs, and time to market.

Obtain / Build

Ensure that service components are available when and where they are needed, and meet agreed specifications.

Deliver & Support

Ensure that services are delivered and supported according to agreed specifications and stakeholders' expectations.

3 The 7 Guiding Principles

1. Focus on value

Everything that the organization does needs to map, directly or indirectly, to value for the stakeholders (Customer Experience - CX).

2. Start where you are

Do not start from scratch and build something new without considering what is already available. Assess the current state objectively.

3. Progress iteratively with feedback

Do not attempt to do everything at once. Organize work into smaller, manageable sections (iterations).

4. Collaborate and promote visibility

Work together across boundaries. Break down silos. Make work, consequences, and current state visible to all.

5. Think and work holistically

No service stands alone. Establish an understanding of how all the parts of an organization work together (Use the 4 Dimensions).

6. Keep it simple and practical

If a process, service, action, or metric provides no value, eliminate it. Use the minimum number of steps necessary.

7. Optimize and automate

Resources of all types should be used to their best effect. Rule: Optimize a process before you automate it with technology. Human intervention should only happen where it contributes value.

5 The Top 7 Key Practices

CRITICAL FOR THE EXAM: Memorize the exact purpose of these 7 practices.

Incident Management

Restore fast

Purpose: To minimize the negative impact of incidents by restoring normal service operation as quickly as possible.

Incident definition: An unplanned interruption to a service or reduction in the quality of a service.

Exam Tip: Swarming (multiple teams working together) is a good technique for complex incidents.

Problem Management

Root causes

Purpose: To reduce the likelihood and impact of incidents by identifying actual and potential causes of incidents, and managing workarounds and known errors.

  • Problem: A cause, or potential cause, of one or more incidents.
  • Known Error: A problem that has been analyzed but not resolved.
  • Workaround: A solution that reduces or eliminates the impact of an incident/problem for which a full resolution is not yet available.

Change Enablement

Maximize successful IT changes

Purpose: To maximize the number of successful service and product changes by ensuring that risks have been properly assessed, authorizing changes to proceed, and managing the change schedule.

Change definition: The addition, modification, or removal of anything that could have a direct or indirect effect on services.

Types of Changes: Standard (pre-authorized, low risk), Normal (requires authorization based on change model), Emergency (must be implemented asap to resolve an incident).

Service Desk

SPOC (Single Point of Contact)

Purpose: To capture demand for incident resolution and service requests. It should also be the entry point and single point of contact for the service provider with all of its users.

Exam Tip: Empathy and communication skills are MORE important than technical skills for a Service Desk agent.

Service Level Management

SLAs & Business view

Purpose: To set clear business-based targets for service levels, and to ensure that delivery of services is properly assessed, monitored, and managed against these targets.

SLA (Service Level Agreement): A documented agreement between a service provider and a customer that identifies both services required and the expected level of service. Must be written in business language, not technical jargon (e.g., "Watermelon SLA" effect).

Service Request Mgmt

User requests

Purpose: To support the agreed quality of a service by handling all pre-defined, user-initiated service requests in an effective and user-friendly manner.

Service Request definition: A request from a user that initiates a service action which has been agreed as a normal part of service delivery (e.g., password reset, request for new software).

Continual Improvement

Everyone's job

Purpose: To align the organization's practices and services with changing business needs through the ongoing improvement of products, services, and practices, or any element involved in the management of products and services.

Important: Continual Improvement is the responsibility of EVERYONE in the organization, not just a dedicated team.
