1 Key Concepts & Definitions

A list of c&d you need to know before you start

Service

A means of enabling valjue co-creation by facilitating outcomes that customer want to achive, without the customer having to manage specific costs and risks.

Value and value co-creation

Value: The perceived benefits, usefulness, and importance of something.

value co-creation: the collaborative process by which service providers, consumers, and other stakeholders jointly create value through the use of products and services, by combining resources, capabilities, and actions across the service relationship to achieve desired outcomes.

Costs & Risks

Cost: The amount of money spent on a specific activity or resource.

Risk: A possible event that could cause harm or loss. Services remove risks from consumers, but also impose new risks on them.

Achieving outcomes involves costs and risks. Service providers help lower the consumer’s costs and risks by leveraging their specialization and economies of scale. However, services can also introduce new costs or risks, so value is only realized if the net effect is positive, meaning that the benefits and removed burdens outweigh any new negatives.

Digital produc

a combination of an organization’s resources based on digital technology and designed to offer value to consumers.

Digital service

a service that fully or largely relies on digital products.

Digital product and service management

a set of specialized organizational capabilities for enabling value for customers in the form of digital products and services.

Product and service management encompass the design, delivery, and continual improvement of digital products and the services based on them. It establishes a common language and conceptual foundation for managing IT-enabled services, focusing not only on the definitions of products and services, but also on how they interact and contribute to value co-creation. This includes a shared understanding of service offerings, service actions, and the roles of providers and consumers withincollaborative service relationships.

Utility, Warranty, user experience and sustainability

Utility (Fit for purpose): The functionality offered by a product or service to meet a particular need. (What the service does).

Warranty (Fit for use): Assurance that a product or service will meet agreed requirements. (Availability, Capacity, Security, Continuity).

User experience: the sum of functional and emotional interactions with a service and service provider as perceived by a service user.

Sustainability: the assurance that a product or service meets and will continue to meet the requirements for environmental stewardship, social progress, and economic growth.

Outputs vs Outcomes

Output: A tangible or intangible deliverable of an activity (e.g., a wedding ring).

Outcome: A result for a stakeholder enabled by one or more outputs (e.g., a happy marriage).

In ITIL, value is always determined by the beneficiary, typically the customer or user, and is achieved through outcomes that meet their needs. A service enables value by helping consumers achieve outcomes, which represent the results they seek, while the service provider takes responsibility for managing the associated costs and risks. Providers produce outputs as part of service delivery, but these outputs do not create value by themselves. Value is realized only when outputs are used effectively to enable the desired outcomes for the service consumer.

Service Offerings

Service offerings describe how an organization presents its services to address the needs of specific consumer groups. They clarify what value is available, how it is accessed, and under what conditions it is provided. By structuring services into clear offerings, organizations enable consistent communication, effective expectation management, and alignment between service design, delivery, and consumption. ITIL advises that service offerings be described from the consumer’s perspective (for example, in a service catalogue), but with clear terms about ownership and responsibilities for any goods or actions involved. Includes:

  • Access to resources: a form of service interaction in which a service consumer is granted the right to use a service provider’s resources under agreed conditions, without transferring ownership.
  • Transfer of goods: a form of service interaction in which a service provider delivers tangible goods to a service consumer, transferring ownership and the associated rights and responsibilities.
  • Service actions: actions performed by the provider to support the needs of the consumer

Goods: tangible resources that are transferred or available for transfer from a service provider to a service consumer, together with ownership and associated rights and responsibilities.

Product: a configuragion of an organization's resources designed to offer value for a customer. Can be used by one or more service offering.

Resource: something you own of have access to. People, information, technology, value streams, processes, suppliers, partners ecc... Resources can be combined toghether to create product delivered as a service to create value for the customers.

Service relationship

Service relationships describe the structured interactions between a service provider and a service consumer that enable ongoing value co-creation. They establish how parties collaborate, communicate, and share responsibilities across the service lifecycle, ensuring that services remain aligned with needs, expectations, and agreed outcomes.

Service journey: the sum of activities and interactions performed by organizations engaged inservice relationships to fulfil their roles as a service provider and a service consumer.

Service quality: the sum of the characteristics of a service that are relevant to its ability to satisfy stated and implied needs.

Service level: a set of metrics that define expected or achieved service quality.

Service Level Agreement (SLA): a documented agreement between a service provider and a customer that identifies the services provided and the agreed level of each service.

Provider & consumer

Service provider: an organization that delivers service to consumer within its own organization or externarl to its own organization.

Service consumer: an organization that uses the service provided to them y any organization with their own organization or external to their organization.

Consumer key roles

  • Sponsor: Authorizes budget for service consumption.
  • Customer: Defines requirements for a service and takes responsibility for outcomes.
  • User: A person who uses services.

Digital product vendor: an organization responsible for the creation and continual improvement of digital products and related service offerings.

Organization: a person or group of people that has its own functions with responsibilities, authorities and relationship to achive its objective. Organizations can be both provider or consumer.

Stakeholders

a person or organization that has an interest or involvement in ah organization, product, service, practice or other entity.

Continual improvement

Show up multple time into ITIL:

PRACTICE:

MODEL: a model providing a structured approach that can be adopted and adapted for implementing improvements.

ACTIVITY: a recurring organizational activity performed at all levels of an organization to ensure that the organization continually meets stakeholders’ expectations.

Lifecycle

the full set of stages, transitions, and associated statuses in the life of a service, product, practice, or other entity.

To ensure quality and continual improvement of digital products and services, organizations must manage products and services throughout their lifecycle. The ITIL Product and Service Lifecycle Model (ITIL PSLM) is a structured model that describes the set of activities an organization uses to conceive, design, create, deliver, operate, and continually improve digital products and services. It shows how products and services evolve over time and how management focus shifts between product development and service delivery to enable ongoing value creation.

2 4 Dimensions of Product and Service Management

The ITIL Four Dimensions of Product and Service Management provide a holistic perspective on how value is created and delivered. They ensure that organizations consider all critical aspects required for effective management, rather than focusing on isolated components or activities.

Organizations & People

this dimension addresses the human side of product and service management. It includes the organizational structure, governance, roles and responsibilities, and the corporate culture. ITIL defines culture as a set of values shared by a group of people, expressed through people’s behaviour and their ideas, beliefs, and practices. An organization must ensure it has the right people, with the appropriate skills and competencies, and that they are organized effectively to achieve the desired outcomes. A key point is that formal structures and processes are not enough; the attitudes and motivations of people (shaped by culture and leadership) significantly affect success.

Information & Technology

this dimension covers the information, data, and technologies utilized and managed by services, as well as those used as part of the organization’s product and service management systems. In a digital service context, information is often one of the most valuable assets (from customer data to metrics, logs, and knowledge bases) and technology is the enabler for creating, storing, processing, and exchanging this information. This dimension also involves information architecture and knowledge management practices to ensure the right data is available to the right people. Moreover, in modern ITIL practice, emerging technologies (like artificial intelligence, machine learning, and automation tools) are leveraged to enhance service delivery and support. The information and technology dimension asks service managers to ensure that their tech stack and data management methods are aligned with the overall needs of the organization and able to support current and future service demands effectively.

Partners & Suppliers

this dimension looks at the relationships an organization has with other entities in order to deliver its products and services. Partners can be other organizations with which one has a close, collaborative relationship in value co-creation. Suppliers are third parties who provide goods or services that the organization needs. The key considerations here include how the organization chooses its suppliers, how it manages and integrates them, and what type of partnerships it cultivates. Different sourcing models (outsourcing, insourcing, multisourcing) and the nature of supplier contracts/agreements will influence product and service management. ITIL highlights that partners and suppliers should be managed to ensure that they contribute to value creation in line with organizational objectives. This includes assessing supplier reliability, clear agreements, and good relationship management.

Value Streams & Processes

this dimension focuses on the workflows and processes the organization uses to create and deliver products and services. A value stream is a series of steps that an organization uses to create and deliver products and services to a service consumer. It represents how value flows through the organization from demand or concept all the way to outcome and value realization. Processes (documented sequences of activities) are building blocks that organizations use to execute parts of value streams. ITIL advocates that organizations understand their value streams and continually analyse and improve them. The key point is to ensure that workflow is effective and efficient: activities across departments should be coordinated, non-value-adding steps removed (as per Lean thinking), and any bottlenecks identified. This dimension also requires considering process controls and governance (policies, roles, and metrics for processes) and process optimization for complexity (since overly rigid processes may fail in complex, rapidly changing environments). By looking at value streams, an organization takes a holistic view of how work is actually done (‘as performed’) versus how it was designed on paper.

3 SERVICE VALUE SYSTEM

Service Value System (SVS)

Describes how all the components and activities of the organization work together as a system to enable value creation.

Input: Opportunity/Demand → Guiding Principles Governance Service Value Chain Practices Continual Improvement → Output: Value

Service Value Chain (SVC)

The central element of the SVS. An operating model which outlines the key activities required to respond to demand and facilitate value realization. (Remember: PIEDOD)

Plan

Ensure a shared understanding of the vision, current status, and improvement direction for all four dimensions.

Improve

Ensure continual improvement of products, services, and practices across all value chain activities.

Engage

Provide a good understanding of stakeholder needs, transparency, and continual engagement (Interacting with users/customers).

Design & Transition

Ensure that products and services continually meet stakeholder expectations for quality, costs, and time to market.

Obtain / Build

Ensure that service components are available when and where they are needed, and meet agreed specifications.

Deliver & Support

Ensure that services are delivered and supported according to agreed specifications and stakeholders' expectations.

The 7 Guiding Principles

1. Focus on value

Everything that the organization does needs to map, directly or indirectly, to value for the stakeholders (Customer Experience - CX).

2. Start where you are

Do not start from scratch and build something new without considering what is already available. Assess the current state objectively.

3. Progress iteratively with feedback

Do not attempt to do everything at once. Organize work into smaller, manageable sections (iterations).

4. Collaborate and promote visibility

Work together across boundaries. Break down silos. Make work, consequences, and current state visible to all.

5. Think and work holistically

No service stands alone. Establish an understanding of how all the parts of an organization work together (Use the 4 Dimensions).

6. Keep it simple and practical

If a process, service, action, or metric provides no value, eliminate it. Use the minimum number of steps necessary.

7. Optimize and automate

Resources of all types should be used to their best effect. Rule: Optimize a process before you automate it with technology. Human intervention should only happen where it contributes value.

Key Practices

CRITICAL FOR THE EXAM: Memorize the exact purpose of these 7 practices.

Incident Management

Restore fast

Purpose: To minimize the negative impact of incidents by restoring normal service operation as quickly as possible.

Incident definition: An unplanned interruption to a service or reduction in the quality of a service.

Exam Tip: Swarming (multiple teams working together) is a good technique for complex incidents.

Problem Management

Root causes

Purpose: To reduce the likelihood and impact of incidents by identifying actual and potential causes of incidents, and managing workarounds and known errors.

  • Problem: A cause, or potential cause, of one or more incidents.
  • Known Error: A problem that has been analyzed but not resolved.
  • Workaround: A solution that reduces or eliminates the impact of an incident/problem for which a full resolution is not yet available.

Change Enablement

Maximize successful IT changes

Purpose: To maximize the number of successful service and product changes by ensuring that risks have been properly assessed, authorizing changes to proceed, and managing the change schedule.

Change definition: The addition, modification, or removal of anything that could have a direct or indirect effect on services.

Types of Changes: Standard (pre-authorized, low risk), Normal (requires authorization based on change model), Emergency (must be implemented asap to resolve an incident).

Service Desk

SPOC (Single Point of Contact)

Purpose: To capture demand for incident resolution and service requests. It should also be the entry point and single point of contact for the service provider with all of its users.

Exam Tip: Empathy and communication skills are MORE important than technical skills for a Service Desk agent.

Service Level Management

SLAs & Business view

Purpose: To set clear business-based targets for service levels, and to ensure that delivery of services is properly assessed, monitored, and managed against these targets.

SLA (Service Level Agreement): A documented agreement between a service provider and a customer that identifies both services required and the expected level of service. Must be written in business language, not technical jargon (e.g., "Watermelon SLA" effect).

Service Request Mgmt

User requests

Purpose: To support the agreed quality of a service by handling all pre-defined, user-initiated service requests in an effective and user-friendly manner.

Service Request definition: A request from a user that initiates a service action which has been agreed as a normal part of service delivery (e.g., password reset, request for new software).

Continual Improvement

Everyone's job

Purpose: To align the organization's practices and services with changing business needs through the ongoing improvement of products, services, and practices, or any element involved in the management of products and services.

Important: Continual Improvement is the responsibility of EVERYONE in the organization, not just a dedicated team.
